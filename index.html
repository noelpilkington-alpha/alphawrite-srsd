  • Verification questions (Step 4): Full in Phases 1-3. Reduced in Phase 4. Absent in Phase 5 (gate).

Note on Sentence Mastery Gate vs. Existing Sentence Mastery Activity

The current Sentence Mastery activity tests production — students write, combine, and transform sentences without scaffolding. The new Sentence Mastery Gate tests discrimination + repair — students identify errors in connected text and fix them. These are complementary skills. Both should exist:

ActivityWhat it testsCognitive demand
Current Sentence MasteryCan you PRODUCE correct sentences?Generate from scratch under varied constraints
NEW Sentence Mastery GateCan you FIND and FIX errors in someone else's writing?Diagnose + repair in context (editing mode)

Students need both because STAAR tests both: the Writing SCR asks students to rewrite/fix sentences (the gate tests this). Production mastery ensures they write correct sentences in their own essays.

Fix Run-Ons & Comma Splices

Node S1 — Sentences (before Combine Sentences) SCAN Before: Combine Sentences

What SRSD Skill Is Being Taught

Skill: Identifying and repairing run-on sentences and comma splices using a named, memorizable procedure (SCAN).

Why it matters:

  • STAAR alignment: The Writing SCR (1 point, every test) asks students to rewrite a flawed sentence. Run-ons and comma splices are the most common error type tested.
  • Placement rationale: Students have just learned all the ways to correctly join clauses (skills 7-10: BBS, subordinating). SCAN teaches them to DIAGNOSE when a join is broken — discrimination before production. They must see what "wrong" looks like before being asked to combine sentences correctly in skill 12.
  • Current challenge it resolves: 5 students in the testing loop are plateaued on sentence-combining after 60-150 reps. They may be producing the very errors they can't diagnose. Teaching error recognition (SCAN) before production (Combine) may break the plateau by building the discrimination foundation that production depends on.
  • Transfer mechanism: SCAN is a named strategy students memorize. On STAAR, when they see a sentence that "sounds wrong," they have a procedure: Stop → Count → Apply → Name. Without SCAN, they rely on intuition (which fails under pressure).

SCAN Strategy

SStop at each period or comma. Is there a full thought on each side?
CCount the independent clauses. More than one with no proper join = problem.
AApply a fix: period (split), semicolon, or comma + conjunction.
NName your fix in your own words ("I split it because..." / "I added 'but' because...").
Step 1 — See It (Discrimination)

Question Type: Multiple-select identification

Student sees 5-6 sentences. They tap ALL sentences that contain a run-on or comma splice. Correct sentences should not be selected.

Model question:

"Tap every sentence below that has a problem. Some are correct — leave those alone."

Sentence 1 — Correct (don't tap)
"She ran fast, and she won the race."
Reason: Two clauses properly joined with comma + coordinating conjunction.
Sentence 2 — Run-on (tap this)
"She ran fast she won the race."
Reason: Two independent clauses with NO join between them.
Sentence 3 — Comma splice (tap this)
"She ran fast, she won the race."
Reason: Two independent clauses joined ONLY by a comma (no conjunction).
Sentence 4 — Correct (don't tap)
"Although the test was hard, most students passed."
Reason: Subordinating conjunction creates a dependent clause — not two independent clauses.
Sentence 5 — Run-on (tap this)
"The movie was great we stayed up late talking about it."
Reason: Two complete thoughts with no punctuation or conjunction between them.

Expected errors & feedback:

Student taps sentence 4 (marks correct sentence as wrong)

▼
Taps "Although the test was hard, most students passed."
This sentence looks like it has two parts, but it's actually correct. "Although the test was hard" starts with "although" — a subordinating conjunction — which makes it a DEPENDENT clause. It can't stand alone. Only join problems happen between two INDEPENDENT clauses (two parts that could each be their own sentence). "Although the test was hard" can't be a sentence by itself, so there's no run-on here.

Student misses sentence 3 (doesn't recognize comma splice)

▼
Correctly taps sentences 2 and 5 but leaves sentence 3 untapped.
You found the run-ons where there's NO punctuation — good eye. But look at sentence 3: "She ran fast, she won the race." There IS a comma, but is a comma ALONE enough to join two complete thoughts? Try the S-test: cover everything after the comma. "She ran fast" — complete thought? Yes. "She won the race" — complete thought? Yes. Two complete thoughts joined only by a comma = comma splice. It needs either a period, a semicolon, or a comma WITH a conjunction (like 'and' or 'so').
Step 2 — Plan It (SCAN Strategy)

Question Type: Structured multi-field — sequential guided questions

One broken sentence is presented. Student works through each SCAN field sequentially. Each field is a specific question with a defined response type.

Sentence presented:

Sentence to fix
"The dog barked loudly the cat ran away."

Field 1: S — Stop (tap interaction)

SQuestion shown: "Read this sentence. Tap where you think one complete thought ENDS and a new one BEGINS."

How student responds: Taps between words in the sentence. The sentence is displayed as tappable word gaps:
The | dog | barked | loudly | ← TAP HERE → | the | cat | ran | away.

Correct response: Tap between "loudly" and "the"

If correct: "Yes! 'The dog barked loudly' is one complete thought. 'The cat ran away' is another. You found the boundary."
If wrong (e.g., taps after "dog"): "Check: is 'The dog' a complete sentence on its own? No — what did the dog do? It's missing the action. Keep reading until you have a full thought that makes sense alone."

Field 2: C — Count (multiple choice)

CQuestion shown: "How many complete thoughts (independent clauses) are in this sentence?"

Choices:
○ 1 complete thought
○ 2 complete thoughts ✓
○ 3 complete thoughts

If correct: "Right — there are 2: 'The dog barked loudly' AND 'the cat ran away.' Both can stand alone."
If wrong (selects 1): "Try reading each side of the boundary you found. 'The dog barked loudly' — could that be its own sentence? Yes. 'The cat ran away' — could that? Yes. That's 2 complete thoughts, not 1."

Field 3: A — Apply (multiple choice, grade-dependent)

AQuestion shown: "How will you fix it? Choose one:"

G3-G5 choices:
○ Split into two sentences (put a period between them)
○ Join with comma + and (the second thought ADDS to the first)
○ Join with comma + but (the second thought CONTRASTS with the first)
○ Join with comma + so (the second thought is the RESULT of the first)

G6-G8 adds:
○ Join with a semicolon (the thoughts are closely related but you don't need a conjunction)

Key design choice: The conjunction options include the RELATIONSHIP in parentheses. This scaffolds the N step — students are already thinking about the relationship when they choose. They don't need to know grammar terms; they just need to identify: "Is the second thought adding something, contrasting, or a result?"

Model correct choice: "Join with comma + so" (the cat running away is the RESULT of the barking)

Field 4: N — Name (scaffolded sentence starter)

NQuestion shown: "Complete this sentence to explain your fix:"

Sentence starter (based on what they selected in A):

If they chose "Split into two sentences":
→ "I split it because these two thoughts are _______________."
Model completion: "separate ideas that don't need to be connected"

If they chose "comma + and":
→ "I used 'and' because the second thought _______________."
Model completion: "adds more information about what happened"

If they chose "comma + but":
→ "I used 'but' because the second thought _______________."
Model completion: "is surprising or different from what you'd expect"

If they chose "comma + so":
→ "I used 'so' because the second thought _______________."
Model completion: "happened as a result of the first thing"

Why sentence starters work: Students don't need to generate the explanation from scratch. The starter provides the grammatical frame; they just complete the reasoning. This is the same principle as Because, But, So — students are extending a stem, not writing from a blank field.

If student writes something incoherent or off-topic:
AI feedback: "Look at the two thoughts: 'The dog barked loudly' and 'the cat ran away.' Did the cat run away AS A RESULT of the barking? Or are these just two separate things that happened? Your answer to that question tells you which conjunction to use."

Expected errors & feedback:

Wrong boundary — student taps after "dog"

▼
Taps between "dog" and "barked" → splitting into "The dog" | "barked loudly the cat ran away."
For S (Stop), you need to find where one COMPLETE thought ends. Test each side: "The dog" — is that a complete sentence on its own? No — what did the dog do? It's missing the action. Keep reading until you have a full thought that works alone as a sentence. Try: "The dog barked loudly" — could THAT be a sentence? Yes! That's where the first thought ends.

Chooses "split" when there's a cause-effect relationship

▼
A: "Split into two sentences." N: "I split it because these two thoughts are separate ideas."
A period IS grammatically correct here. But read the two thoughts together: "The dog barked loudly. The cat ran away." Does the order matter? Did the cat run away BECAUSE of the barking? If so, there's a cause-and-result relationship — the second thing happened because of the first. When thoughts have that kind of connection, a conjunction like 'so' keeps the relationship visible to the reader: "The dog barked loudly, so the cat ran away." A period isn't wrong, but it loses the connection. Which version helps the reader understand WHY the cat ran?

Chooses wrong conjunction (e.g., "but" for a cause-effect)

▼
A: "Join with comma + but." N: "I used 'but' because the second thought is different."
Think about what 'but' means: it signals CONTRAST or SURPRISE. "The dog barked loudly, BUT the cat ran away" implies it's SURPRISING that the cat ran. Is that surprising? Not really — loud barking would naturally make a cat run. The relationship here is RESULT, not contrast: the barking CAUSED the running. That's 'so': "The dog barked loudly, SO the cat ran away." Try reading both versions out loud — which one matches what actually happened?

N step — student can't complete the sentence starter

▼
Chose "comma + so" but for N writes: "I used 'so' because the second thought... I don't know."
Let's think about it together. You chose 'so' — that means you sensed the second thought is a RESULT of the first. Let's check: What happened first? (The dog barked loudly.) What happened second? (The cat ran away.) Did the second thing happen BECAUSE of the first thing? If yes, then you can write: "I used 'so' because the cat ran away as a result of the loud barking." Try completing it: "I used 'so' because the second thought happened because of ___."
Step 3 — Do It (Production — GRADED)

Question Type: Text input — rewrite the sentence

Student types the corrected sentence using their chosen fix from Step 2. Their SCAN plan is visible for reference.

Model answer:

Student writes
"The dog barked loudly, so the cat ran away."

AI grading criteria:

  • Is the sentence grammatically correct? (no run-on, no splice remaining)
  • Is the meaning preserved from the original?
  • Does the fix match what the student planned in Step 2 (A field)?

Expected errors & feedback:

Adds comma without conjunction (creates comma splice)

▼
"The dog barked loudly, the cat ran away."
You added a comma — but a comma ALONE can't join two independent clauses. That's the exact error we're trying to fix (a comma splice). In your A step, you chose "comma + conjunction." You have the comma, but where's the conjunction? Add a word like 'so', 'and', or 'but' after the comma: "The dog barked loudly, so the cat ran away."

Uses conjunction without comma

▼
"The dog barked loudly so the cat ran away."
Good choice of conjunction — 'so' shows cause-and-effect. But when joining two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction (and, but, so, or, yet), you need a comma BEFORE the conjunction: "The dog barked loudly, so the cat ran away." The comma signals to the reader: "a new independent clause is coming."

Changes meaning with wrong conjunction

▼
"The dog barked loudly, but the cat ran away."
Grammatically correct — two clauses properly joined. But does "but" capture the right relationship? "But" means contrast/surprise. Was it surprising that the cat ran away from loud barking? Probably not — running away is the EXPECTED result. 'So' (result) or 'and' (addition) would preserve the original meaning better. Think about your N step: what's the relationship between these thoughts?
Step 4 — Verify It (Targeted Verification Questions)

Question Type: Specific questions about their fix — mix of typing and multiple choice

Instead of generic checkboxes, students answer questions about their OWN fix. Some require typing (forces re-reading), some are MC (reduces frustration for students who struggle to articulate). The AI compares answers to the submitted fix.

Verification Question 1 (type response)
"Type the FIRST complete thought from your fixed sentence."
Student types: "The dog barked loudly"
AI checks: Yes — "The dog barked loudly" has a subject (dog) and a verb (barked). It works as its own sentence. ✓

If student types something incomplete (e.g., "The dog"):
"That's not a complete thought yet — what did the dog DO? Keep going until you have a subject + verb that makes sense alone."

If student types the whole fixed sentence (e.g., "The dog barked loudly, so the cat ran away"):
"That's your whole sentence, not just the FIRST complete thought. Find where one thought ends: 'The dog barked loudly' — stop there. That's thought #1."
Verification Question 2 (type response)
"Type the SECOND complete thought from your fixed sentence."
Student types: "the cat ran away"
AI checks: Yes — "the cat ran away" has a subject (cat) and a verb (ran). It works as its own sentence. ✓

If student includes the conjunction (e.g., "so the cat ran away"):
"You included 'so' — that's the conjunction that CONNECTS the two thoughts, but it's not part of the second thought itself. The second complete thought is 'the cat ran away.' The 'so' just tells the reader about the relationship between them."
Verification Question 3 (multiple choice — scaffolded)
"What is the RELATIONSHIP between these two thoughts?"

Choices:

  • ○ The second thought ADDS more information (and)
  • ○ The second thought is SURPRISING or OPPOSITE (but)
  • ● The second thought is a RESULT of the first (so) ✓
  • ○ The two thoughts are UNRELATED (period — separate sentences)
If correct (selects "result"): "Right — the cat ran away BECAUSE of the barking. Your fix uses 'so,' which signals a result. The meaning is preserved. ✓"

If wrong (e.g., selects "adds more"): "Think about it: did the cat running away just happen to occur at the same time as the barking? Or did the barking CAUSE the cat to run? If the barking caused it, that's a result relationship — 'so.' If you used 'and' in your fix, it would work grammatically but lose the cause-and-effect meaning."

If selects "unrelated": "If these thoughts were truly unrelated, the writer wouldn't have put them next to each other. Read them together: a dog barked loudly... a cat ran away. Is there a connection? Most readers would see cause-and-effect: the loud bark scared the cat. If your fix was a period (two separate sentences), you're not wrong grammatically — but you lost that connection for the reader."

Why this design works:

  • Q1 and Q2 (typing) force students to re-read their fix and identify the two independent clauses. If they can't, the AI catches it before grading: "You couldn't identify the two thoughts — that means your fix might still be broken."
  • Q3 (multiple choice) avoids the "I don't know how to answer" problem. Students don't need to articulate the relationship from scratch — they select from labeled options that include the plain-English meaning (ADDS, SURPRISING, RESULT, UNRELATED). This matches how Because, But, So was taught: students already know cause/contrast/result from BBS — Q3 just asks them to apply that knowledge.
  • The Q3 options mirror the A step options: "and" = adds, "but" = surprising/contrast, "so" = result, "period" = unrelated. If their Q3 answer doesn't match their A step choice, the AI surfaces the mismatch: "You chose 'and' (adds) in your fix, but you just said the relationship is 'result.' Those don't match — the conjunction should signal the actual relationship. Would 'so' be a better choice?"

Sentence Mastery Gate

Sentences Skill 16 (Final) No scaffold All Sentences skills mastered

What SRSD Skill Is Being Taught

Skill: Independent error diagnosis and repair in connected text — finding and fixing sentence-level errors in a full paragraph without any strategy labels, hints, or scaffolding.

Why it matters:

  • Complements existing Sentence Mastery activity: The current mastery tests PRODUCTION (write/combine/transform sentences). This gate tests DISCRIMINATION + REPAIR (find and fix errors in context). Both are needed: STAAR tests both.
  • Tests transfer to context: Students may fix a run-on in isolation but miss it inside a paragraph where meaning flows across the error. This tests whether error-identification skills work in connected text.
  • Prerequisite validation: This gate proves students can independently apply sentence skills before advancing to Paragraphs. Without it, scaffold-dependent mastery at the sentence level propagates upward — students write paragraphs with sentence errors they can't catch.
  • One shot, no revision: Mirrors test conditions. The first draft is the score. This is the first time students encounter "no revision" in the platform — preparing them for the Paragraph and ECR Mastery Gates.

Gate Design

One step. No SCAN labels. No hints. No tutorial. No revision loop.
Student sees a paragraph with embedded errors and must fix ALL of them in one pass.

Write Supporting Details

Paragraphs Node S2 PROVE After: Finishing Transition Words | Before: CASE

What SRSD Skill Is Being Taught

Skill: Developing a single supporting detail fully — citing passage evidence, paraphrasing it, explaining its significance, and connecting it back to the main point.

Why it matters:

  • The #1 coaching observation: Elaboration ("the so what") is the first thing that vanishes under multi-paragraph load. Students give a detail but don't explain why it matters. This activity isolates that specific skill and gives it a mandatory, named step (V = Very important because).
  • The only paragraph element not taught in isolation: AlphaWrite teaches topic sentences (skills 5-7), sequencing (skill 3), relevance (skill 4), transitions (skills 13-14), and outlining (skills 9-11). It does NOT explicitly teach "how to cite evidence from a passage and explain its significance." PROVE fills that gap.
  • STAAR Trait 1 — depth of thought: The difference between Trait 1 Score 2 and Score 3 is "depth" — explained evidence versus merely stated evidence. PROVE directly trains the Score 3 behavior.
  • Passage-based from day one: Like Write a Paragraph from Prompt, students choose a topic they love and the system generates a passage about it. Students still write about what they care about — but they practice the specific skill of citing FROM the passage text and explaining why it matters. This builds the passage-referencing habit that STAAR demands without breaking the "write what they love" mandate.
  • Feeds into CASE/FACT: Each reason in CASE (the A — Argue) and each detail in FACT (the A — Add details) is internally developed using PROVE. Learning PROVE first means students have the detail-level procedure before encountering the paragraph/essay-level structure.

PROVE Strategy

PPoint — What are you trying to show? (connects to your topic sentence)
RReference — What does the passage say? (find a specific detail)
OOwn words — Paraphrase it (don't copy the passage word-for-word)
VVery important because — Explain WHY this matters (the "so what")
EEnd link — Connect back to your point (how does this support your claim?)
Step 1 — See It (Discrimination)

Question Type: Side-by-side comparison + reason selection

Student sees two versions of the same supporting detail — controlled for length — and identifies which does a better job PROVING the point. Length is matched so students must evaluate quality, not quantity.

Model question:

"Both responses try to support the point 'Dogs are loyal.' They are about the same length. Which one does a better job PROVING the point? Then select WHY."

Version A (~50 words)
"Dogs are loyal animals. The passage mentions that dogs wait by the door. Dogs are really good pets and lots of people love having them. They are friendly and always want to play with their owners. Many families choose dogs because they are so loyal."
Version B (~50 words)
"Dogs are loyal. According to the passage, dogs wait by the door for hours when their owners leave. This shows that dogs form real emotional bonds — they remember and miss people even when they can't see them. This kind of devotion proves dogs are among the most loyal animals."

Both are ~50 words. The difference is NOT length — it's what they do with the words. Version A fills space with generic claims. Version B develops one specific detail with explanation.

Student selects why B is better (multiple correct):

  • ☑ "Uses a SPECIFIC detail from the passage — not just 'mentions' it but adds the specifics (for hours, when owners leave)" (R)
  • ☑ "Explains WHY the detail matters — what does waiting by the door actually prove?" (V)
  • ☑ "Connects back to the main point about loyalty at the end" (E)
  • ☐ "Is longer" (NOT valid — they're the same length)
  • ☐ "Uses bigger or better vocabulary" (NOT valid — vocabulary isn't the difference here)

What makes Version A weak despite being the same length:

  • Vague passage reference: "mentions that dogs wait by the door" (no specifics — wait how long? when? why does that matter?)
  • No explanation of significance: States the fact and immediately moves on to unrelated claims
  • Filler that doesn't prove the point: "good pets," "people love them," "like to play" — none of this specifically proves LOYALTY
  • Circular ending: "families choose dogs because they are loyal" just restates the claim without developing it

Expected errors & feedback:

Student selects Version A as better

▼
Selects A, possibly because it "mentions more things about dogs" or "talks about how people love dogs."
Version A says several nice things about dogs — they're friendly, people love them, they like to play. But does any of that specifically prove LOYALTY? Being friendly isn't the same as being loyal. Playing isn't loyalty. Version B focuses on ONE specific behavior — waiting by the door for hours — and then explains exactly WHY that proves loyalty: "they remember and miss people even when they can't see them." A detail that goes DEEP on one specific point is stronger than a response that goes wide on many unrelated points. Ask yourself: which version would actually convince someone that dogs are loyal?

Student correctly picks B but selects "uses bigger words" as reason

▼
Picks B (correct) but selects "Uses bigger or better vocabulary" as the reason why.
It's not about vocabulary — both versions use simple words. Look at what each version DOES with the passage detail:

• Version A: "The passage mentions that dogs wait by the door." → Then? Nothing about it. Moves to a different topic (playing, being friendly).
• Version B: "Dogs wait by the door for hours..." → "This SHOWS that dogs form real emotional bonds" → "This PROVES they are loyal."

B takes the detail and does two things with it: EXPLAINS what it means (V step) and CONNECTS it back to the point (E step). A just states the detail and abandons it. That's the difference — explanation and connection, not vocabulary.

Question Type: Structured multi-field input (passage-based)

Student reads a passage, then fills in each PROVE field to plan one supporting detail.

Residents reported feeling safer and more connected to their neighbors. People who had never spoken to each other started conversations while working side by side in the garden beds, building friendships that extended beyond the garden fence.

Write a Paragraph — Opinion (CASE)

Paragraphs Node S3 (NEW — before Write from Prompt) CASE After: PROVE | Before: Write a Paragraph from Prompt (existing mastery check)

What SRSD Skill Is Being Taught

Skill: Planning and writing an opinion paragraph from a passage-based prompt using a memorizable strategy (CASE = "make your case") that connects planning directly to production within a single workflow.

Why it matters:

  • The core diagnosis: AlphaWrite teaches planning (SPO, skills 9-10) and writing (Turn Outline into Draft, skill 11; Write from Prompt) but the planning activities and writing activities are SEPARATE. Students never use their own plan to write their own draft in one connected workflow. CASE fixes this: plan and draft happen in the same activity, with the plan visible during drafting.
  • How CASE complements SPO: SPO (skills 9-10) teaches the CONCEPT of paragraph structure — what a plan looks like, how to create one. CASE gives students a MEMORABLE DEPLOYMENT PROCEDURE for that concept — a named strategy they can retrieve from memory on a test. SPO = "paragraphs have topic sentences, details, and conclusions." CASE = "when I sit down to write my opinion, I make my C-A-S-E." SPO is the knowledge; CASE is the automatic procedure for using that knowledge.
  • A real word that names the genre: CASE isn't an arbitrary acronym — when you write an opinion, you are literally "making a case" for what you think. The word itself signals the task, which makes it more memorable and helps students recognize when to reach for it.
  • Relationship to Write a Paragraph from Prompt: CASE is the SCAFFOLDED practice activity. Write a Paragraph from Prompt remains as the UNSCAFFOLDED mastery check. Students learn the strategy with support in CASE, then prove they can deploy it independently in Write from Prompt (where no CASE scaffold is visible). Sequence: learn with scaffold → practice with scaffold → prove without scaffold.
  • The mnemonic that scales — and upgrades: CASE at paragraph level (C = claim, A = argue/reasons, S = support/evidence, E = end) scales to essay level (C = thesis/intro, A = body paragraphs, S = support within each, E = conclusion). At G6-8 it UPGRADES to DARE (adds R = reject the other side) — see the STOP+DARE tab. Students learn ONE strategy that scales and then gains one new move.
  • STAAR alignment: Opinion/Argumentative ECR is one of the available modes at every grade (G3-8). Worth 10 points. CASE directly teaches the organizational structure that earns Trait 1 Score 2-3.

CASE Strategy

CClaim — State your opinion in one clear sentence.
AArgue — List your reasons WHY you think this (2 reasons). Just the reasons — you'll develop them next.
SSupport — For EACH reason: What does the passage say that supports it? And WHY does that matter? (This is where PROVE lives.)
EEnd — Wrap it up. Don't just repeat your claim — add a final thought.

The logic: C = state your position. A = brainstorm your reasons (the "what"). S = develop each reason with evidence and explanation (the "proof"). E = close. Students think in stages: position first, then reasons, then development, then closure.

Write a Paragraph — Informational (FACT)

Paragraphs Node S4 FACT After: CASE (Opinion)

What SRSD Skill Is Being Taught

Skill: Planning and writing an informational/explanatory paragraph from a passage-based prompt using FACT — a strategy for organizing factual explanation that is distinct from opinion writing (CASE).

Why it matters:

  • Genre confusion is a real failure mode: Students who have only practiced CASE (opinion) will write opinions when the prompt asks them to EXPLAIN. STAAR prompts like "Explain how the author shows..." require informational writing — no opinion needed. Students must learn to recognize the prompt type and switch strategies.
  • STAAR Informational ECR: Tested at every grade (G3-8), worth 10 points. Requires the student to explain what the passage says and why it matters — NOT state what they think. A different cognitive act from argumentation.
  • Heavier passage dependency: In CASE, the student's opinion drives the paragraph and the passage provides support. In FACT, the passage's content drives the paragraph and the student provides explanation. This trains a different relationship with the text — more reading comprehension integrated with writing.
  • Builds on PROVE: FACT's C step (Clarify) is PROVE applied to informational detail. Students who mastered PROVE already know how to develop a detail — now they do it within an informational frame rather than an argumentative one.
  • Introduces genre discrimination: This is the first time students must distinguish between prompt types (opinion vs. informational) and deploy the correct strategy. CASE came first (opinion). Now they learn FACT (informational) and practice switching between them.
  • Same relationship to Write from Prompt as CASE: FACT is scaffolded practice. Write from Prompt (existing) is the unscaffolded mastery check where students prove they can write informational paragraphs without FACT fields visible.

FACT Strategy

FFocus — What are you explaining? (the controlling idea, from the passage/prompt, NOT your opinion)
AAdd details — List the key facts from the passage (2-3). Just the facts — you'll develop them next.
CClarify — For EACH detail: explain it further. Why is it important? What does it mean? What would happen without it?
TTie up — Conclude, connecting back to the focus. What's the big picture?

The logic: F = state the focus. A = add the key facts (brainstorm stage — just list them). C = clarify each fact with explanation (depth stage — go deeper). T = tie it up. Like CASE, students think in stages: focus first, then facts, then development, then closure.

Step 1 — See It (Two-Part Discrimination)

Part A: Genre discrimination — this is where students learn to switch between CASE and FACT

Now that students know CASE (opinion), they must learn to recognize when a prompt asks for something DIFFERENT. This is the first time they encounter the choice.

The discrimination prompt: "Is this prompt asking you to make a CASE (your opinion) or give the FACTS (explain something)?"

Prompt A
"Do you think students should have longer recess? Use evidence from the passage."
→ Opinion → CASE ("Do you think" = your opinion)
Prompt B
"Explain how bees help plants grow. Use details from the passage."
→ Informational → FACT ("Explain how" = describe a process from the passage)
Prompt C
"Describe the steps an astronaut takes to prepare for a spacewalk. Use information from the passage."
→ Informational → FACT ("Describe the steps" = explain information, not state an opinion)

The key discrimination: Does the prompt ask what YOU THINK? → make a CASE. Does the prompt ask you to EXPLAIN what the passage SAYS? → give the FACTS.

Argument Essay — STOP + DARE (G6-8)

Node S11 (G6-8 — upgrades CASE) STOP + DARE After: student owns CASE | The one cross-band mnemonic upgrade

What SRSD Skill Is Being Taught

Skill: Planning and writing a multi-paragraph ARGUMENT at G6-8 using STOP (the planning phase) and DARE (the draft structure) — adding the one structural move G8 Score 3 requires that CASE doesn't have: counterargument + refutation.

Why it matters:

  • The one place a mnemonic differentiates across grade bands. G3-5 opinion = CASE (no counterargument — correct for the band). G6-8 argument = STOP+DARE. Every other mnemonic (FACT, PROVE, COPS, SCAN) is constant across all grades. Opinion is the only one that upgrades, because counterargument is the only genuinely new structural move in the whole G3-8 sequence.
  • G8 Score 3 REQUIRES counterargument identified AND refuted. CASE tops out at Score 2 on a G8 argumentative prompt by construction — it has no "address the other side" move. DARE's R is exactly that move.
  • Research-validated. STOP+DARE is the SRSD persuasive-writing strategy (De La Paz & Graham, 1997; Graham & Harris), studied with middle-school students including struggling writers — exactly this band. Students taught it include counterarguments far more often than controls.
  • Why introduce at G6, not G8: Counterargument becomes scoring-critical at G8, but you must not introduce a brand-new strategy in the highest-stakes year. G6 + G7 give students time to automate it before G8 makes it count.
  • Why argument planning gets its OWN mnemonic (STOP) but informational doesn't: Argument planning is a genuine two-beat act — weigh both sides, THEN commit. A single letter can't honestly carry "brainstorm both sides" AND "commit to a thesis." Informational planning is one beat (decide your focus), so it lives inside FACT's F. The principle: planning earns its own mnemonic only when planning is itself a multi-beat generative act.

The Two Mnemonics

STOP — the PLANNING phase (what you do before drafting):

SSuspend judgment — brainstorm what BOTH sides would say (don't pick yet)
TTake a side — now choose your position
OOrganize — pick your strongest points + note which opposing points you'll answer
PPlan — keep planning as you write

DARE — the DRAFT structure (the essay itself):

DDevelop your thesis — intro paragraph
AAdd support — reasons + evidence + explanation (body paragraphs; PROVE lives here)
RReject the other side — state the counterargument AND refute it (counterargument paragraph)
EEnd — conclusion paragraph

How STOP feeds DARE: the opposing arguments a student surfaces in STOP's S (Suspend judgment / brainstorm both sides) become exactly what they refute in DARE's R. The planning brainstorm is what makes the counterargument honest rather than a token.

The CASE → DARE Upgrade (taught explicitly)

DARE is the middle-school version of CASE — same backbone, one new move

Student-facing framing: "DARE is the middle-school version of CASE. You still argue your reasons with evidence (now together as 'Add support') and End strong — plus one powerful new move: R, take on the people who disagree and beat them."

Because the student already knows how to take on a strategy (they own CASE), adopting the upgraded one is fast — they're learning ONE new move (R) and a planning phase (STOP), not a whole new system.

Worked Example — G8 Argument with Counterargument (DARE's R in action)
"Some experts argue competitive gaming should count as a real sport; others disagree. Using both passages, argue your position." (Paired passages: one on esports training/athleticism; one on traditional-sport physicality.)
Strong G8 response — earns Trait 1 Score 3

[D — Intro / thesis]
Competitive gaming should count as a real sport because it demands the same training, skill, and competition as traditional sports.

[A — Body 1, evidence from Passage 1]
Professional gamers train 8-10 hours a day, just like athletes. According to Passage 1, top esports players follow strict practice schedules, study opponents, and drill reaction times — the same dedication a basketball player brings to the court.

[A — Body 2, evidence from Passage 1]
Esports also requires elite skill and teamwork. Passage 1 explains that winning teams coordinate split-second strategies under pressure, which is exactly the kind of high-level decision-making that defines competitive sport.

[R — Counterargument paragraph, drawing on Passage 2]
Some argue that a real sport must involve physical exertion, and gaming is sedentary. But Passage 2 itself notes that shooting and archery are Olympic sports with minimal physical strain. If those count, then physical exertion isn't what defines a sport — skill and competition are. The "it's not physical enough" argument doesn't hold.

[E — Conclusion]
Because gaming demands the training, skill, and competition of any traditional sport — and the one objection doesn't survive scrutiny — competitive gaming deserves to be called a real sport.

Why this scores 3 on Trait 1 at G8: clear thesis (D); evidence from BOTH passages (A pulls Passage 1, R pulls Passage 2 — the paired-passage Score-3 requirement); counterargument IDENTIFIED and REFUTED (R — the G8-specific requirement); organized intro/body/counter/conclusion; depth via PROVE inside each A paragraph.

Edit a Paragraph

Paragraphs Node S7 COPS After: CASE & FACT | Before: the mastery checks

What SRSD Skill Is Being Taught

Skill: Systematic proofreading — finding and fixing convention errors in written text using a named, category-based procedure (COPS).

The detective framing (how COPS is taught in direct instruction)
COPS is the one acronym in the set that isn't a dictionary word — so we make it a vivid ROLE the student plays: "You are the COPS. Investigate your own writing and catch the wrongdoers — the errors. A good investigator doesn't stop at the first clue; they search the whole scene systematically." This turns the weakness (not a real word) into a strength: COPS = police = a detective hunting for what's wrong. The "thorough investigation" frame directly teaches the behavior COPS exists to instill — students stop proofreading too early, so framing it as a systematic search (check the whole scene, all four categories) counters that. Keep it positive: the student is the detective; the errors are what they're hunting (never about catching people).

Why it matters:

  • Students don't proofread: Direct coaching observation reveals students write, stop, and submit — no re-reading, no self-correction. COPS gives them a procedure for what "checking your work" actually means.
  • STAAR ECR Trait 2 (Conventions): 0-2 points. Students who produce good content (Trait 1 ≥ 1) but have convention errors lose these points unnecessarily. COPS targets exactly this: catch errors AFTER producing content.
  • Self-regulation for conventions: Currently the AI catches errors and tells students what to fix (external regulation). COPS teaches students to catch their OWN errors (self-regulation). On STAAR, no AI exists — students who have internalized COPS self-check; students who relied on AI feedback don't.
  • Different cognitive mode from writing: Editing requires "reading like a reader" (looking for errors) rather than "reading like a writer" (thinking about content). Teaching it separately, after CASE/FACT, helps students understand that WRITING and EDITING are two different phases — write first, edit after.
  • Placement — after CASE/FACT, BEFORE the mastery checks: COPS comes after content is established (PROVE/CASE/FACT) but before the unscaffolded mastery checks (Write a Paragraph from Prompt, Free-Form, Gate). Two reasons. (1) Content outranks conventions: the STAAR rubric auto-zeros Trait 2 (conventions) when Trait 1 (development) is absent — so teaching proofreading before students can produce content would have them polishing paragraphs that score zero. (2) The mastery checks must certify a proofread paragraph: if COPS came after them, students would certify "can write independently" without ever having learned to self-check. By teaching COPS first, every mastery check from Write a Paragraph from Prompt onward includes a COPS self-check, so proofreading becomes a habit attached to all independent production — not a skill bolted on at the very end. Note: the Edit-a-Paragraph activity operates on a PROVIDED paragraph with embedded errors, so it does not require the student's own prior production to function.
  • Relationship to SCAN: COPS is the general proofreading pass; SCAN is the specific fix for one error type (run-ons/comma splices) a student may invoke during COPS's P (Punctuation) check. SCAN nests inside COPS.

CCapitalization — First word of sentences. Proper nouns. Titles.

  • The largest single STAAR point opportunity: ECR = 10 points. The rubric requires intro + body + conclusion even at G3. Students who write one paragraph — however excellent — cap at Trait 1 Score 1 (= 2 raw points). Multi-paragraph structure alone moves students to Score 2 (= 4 raw points). +2-4 points per student.
  • CASE scales — but the transition must be explicitly taught: Students already know CASE for paragraphs. Now they learn that CASE ALSO works for essays, but each letter gets BIGGER. The Step 1 discrimination explicitly shows paragraph-CASE vs. essay-CASE side by side, so students understand the scaling — it's not assumed.
  • Solves the elaboration collapse: When students attempted multi-paragraph writing on assessments, elaboration vanished — not because they can't elaborate, but because organizational demands consumed their working memory. The essay-level CASE plan externalizes organization → frees bandwidth for paragraph-level quality (including PROVE within each body paragraph).
  • Multi-session design: At 20-25 minutes per session, students may need 2 sessions. Session 1: read + plan (CASE). Session 2: write (with plan visible). The saved CASE plan eliminates working-memory loss between days.

Content Dial — Interest-Adjacent Content Extension

Cross-cutting content design (applies to all post-S8 composition activities) Two-Dial Model Separate research track — see the Two-Dial BrainLift

What This Is & Why It Lives Here

The SRSD strategy work (CASE, FACT, PROVE, STOP+DARE, COPS) closes the process transfer gap — can a student deploy a strategy without a scaffold? This feature closes a different, content-side gap — students write fluently about topics they know well, then collapse when a test hands them unfamiliar material.

"Write what they love" — AlphaWrite's motivational foundation — can inadvertently manufacture this gap: students never practice the cognitive condition the test demands (grappling with less-familiar content). Interest-adjacent content extension closes the gap without abandoning the motivation that makes the program work.

Why this is on its own tab (and its own BrainLift)
This mechanism warrants its own research base (Ausubel on prior knowledge, McCutchen on domain-knowledge load, the HS Brainlift's PROCESS-vs-CONTENT split) and its own rollout. It has been removed from the SRSD Integration BrainLift and split into a dedicated Two-Dial BrainLift. This tab is the developer-facing version of that design so it stays visible to the build team. Nothing in the core SRSD build depends on this being live — it is an additive second lever. Ship the strategy instruction on familiar/adjacent content first.

The Two-Dial Model — The Governing Rule

There are two independent difficulty dials. Never turn both up in the same step.

1Scaffold fade — full → labels → none (the existing fade architecture)
2Content difficulty — familiar core → adjacent → genuine fringe

When content difficulty goes up, the scaffold stays put. When the scaffold comes off, content holds. They alternate, so every step introduces only ONE new difficulty:

StageDial 1 (Scaffold)Dial 2 (Content)
Learn CASE/FACTFullFamiliar core
Later scaffolded practiceLabelsAdjacent (content extension introduced here)
Write a Paragraph from PromptNoneAdjacent (held)
ECR Mastery GateNoneFringe (turned up again) = full STAAR simulation
The Core Move

A student chooses an interest (Minecraft, dogs, Fortnite). Instead of letting them write about the familiar core of that interest, the system extends them into an adjacent context within it — where the academic domain arrives wearing the costume of the interest. The student thinks they're still writing about Minecraft; they're actually writing about physics. Motivation stays high (it's still their interest); content genuinely challenges them (less-familiar territory they must grapple with).

The load-bearing element: the bridging passage
For novices, unfamiliar content makes writing WORSE (Ausubel; McCutchen) — UNLESS the missing domain knowledge is supplied. The bridging passage does exactly that: it converts "write about something you don't know" (a load spike the research forbids) into "read this, then write about it" (the exact STAAR task format). The bridging passage is not a safety feature — it is what makes content extension legitimate at every grade.

What the worked examples reveal:

  • Different interests ramp toward different academic domains (Minecraft→physics/CS, dogs→biology, Fortnite→economics/ethics). A student's interest portfolio naturally exposes them to multiple domains without ever leaving "stuff I like."
  • Most interests bridge to a recurring set of domains — physics, biology, economics, psychology/ethics, CS. The generator can use pre-built domain-bridge templates rather than inventing from scratch (more reliable, more testable).
  • Niche/"fluffy" interests often ramp BETTER than popular ones — "my pet axolotl" → regeneration biology + conservation ethics; "slime" → polymer chemistry / non-Newtonian fluids. Assume every interest has a domain bridge until proven otherwise; do NOT penalize obscure interests.
  • The shallow-ceiling failure pattern is real but bounded — narrow single-object interests (puppies) top out early. Handled by the widening rule.

The Generation Risk — the make-or-break engineering concern

The entire quality of this feature lives in the prompt + bridging-passage generation. A bad generator produces fringe topics that are either too far (no foothold → anxiety spike, the exact failure the research warns of) or fake-fringe (a lateral move with no added rigor). Five generation rules guard against this:

  1. Cross a different domain, not just a sub-topic. ("Favorite Minecraft mod" = fake fringe; "how Minecraft generates worlds" = real fringe into CS.) Test: name the academic domain; if you can't, regenerate.
  2. Visible causal bridge from interest to domain. Test: "Because you like [interest], you've probably wondered [fringe question]." If forced, the bridge is broken.
  3. Distance calibrated by the passage, not the prompt (the grade-amplitude dial).
  4. The bridging passage is self-sufficient — supplies all domain knowledge needed; a student who knows only their interest can write a passing response from the passage alone.
  5. Tag the genre (opinion → CASE/DARE vs informational → FACT) so the offered choices can include both and reinforce genre discrimination.
Validation gate before committing fringe as the default diet
Run the generator on the top ~30 real student interests (from dashboard usage data) at G3, G5, and G8. Human-rate each on four binary checks — crosses a domain? bridge intact? passage self-sufficient? grade-calibrated? Pass bar: ≥80% score 4/4. Below that, generation is not reliable enough to make fringe the default — ship it as an opt-in stretch until generation improves. "Mostly fringe" with a bad generator becomes "mostly frustration."

Rollout: broad as the target, contained as the pilot

Target design (ambient): content extension as a mode across all post-eligibility composition activities, amplitude governed by the two-dial rule, with one explicit on-ramp activity (fringe introduced deliberately, full scaffold, smallest step, maximum passage support — so the student learns "writing about adjacent content" as its own move before it becomes the default).

Pilot (contained): do NOT turn it on everywhere at once — you couldn't then isolate its effect. Introduce it contained (on-ramp + one or two activities), measure that cohort against a familiar-content control, and expand to ambient once the data confirms it closes the transfer gap. Prove it works before betting the whole composition sequence on it.

Future Capability — NOT in current scope

A stronger version would be a per-student, recoverable content dial: when a response has sound structure but thin content, the next prompt drops one notch closer to familiar; on success it climbs back up — so each student oscillates around their own zone of proximal development (an off-day student gets a recoverable win; a fringe-too-far student settles at their real edge).

This depends on dynamic per-student difficulty adjustment that the linear progression model does not currently support. Recorded here so the idea is preserved for if/when the ecosystem gains adaptive routing.

SRSD Integration BrainLift — Full Document

The complete strategy-instruction BrainLift: Purpose, Experts (DOK 1), Spiky POVs (DOK 4), Knowledge Tree (DOK 2), Key Sources, Research Insights (DOK 3), and Cross-Source Synthesis. All citations verified against primary sources.

Purpose

The purpose of this BrainLift is to define how Self-Regulated Strategy Development (SRSD) integrates into AlphaWrite's existing TWR-based curriculum to close the transfer gap between in-app performance and assessment performance. It rests on one core diagnosis: AlphaWrite teaches the components of writing effectively in its scaffolded environment, but it does not teach the self-management students need to deploy those components independently under test conditions.

The core principles:

  • Teaching a cognitively demanding strategy is not the same as getting a student to own it. Ownership requires memorization, repeated deployment, scaffold fading, and unscaffolded performance.
  • Cognitively demanding scaffolds that live in the environment (the app) do not transfer to new environments (the test) when the two environments differ too much. Only strategies the student has memorized can travel.
  • Planning must connect to production within the same workflow, in a way that is memorable, before students will experience its value.
  • Self-regulation (self-monitoring, self-evaluation) replaces external regulation (AI feedback) as the mechanism for writing quality on assessments, where no AI feedback follows each production rep.
  • The "discrimination before production" principle applies to strategy use itself. Students must first see what planned versus unplanned writing looks like before they can value the planning strategy being taught.

In Scope:

  • SRSD integration with TWR's existing sentence-paragraph-essay progression
  • Genre-specific mnemonic selection and deployment for standardized writing modes
  • Scaffold fading methodology (visible, then reduced, then absent, then gate)
  • Plan-to-draft connection within AlphaWrite's platform constraints
  • Transfer mechanism from app practice to unscaffolded assessment performance. This covers the process gap: deploying a memorized strategy without a scaffold.
  • Self-regulation instruction within an AI-graded revision loop

Out of Scope:

  • SRSD for populations with learning disabilities (separate research base; findings included only where generalizable)
  • Classroom-based SRSD delivery
  • The full writing motivation architecture (addressed in the main Writing Brainlift; this Brainlift focuses on strategy instruction)
  • The content transfer gap, meaning writing about less-familiar material, and the two independent difficulty dials (scaffold versus content) that govern it. This warrants its own research base and is addressed in a separate BrainLift (see two-difficulty-dials).
  • Sentence-level skill instruction (existing AlphaWrite courses are strong here; this document addresses what comes after)

Main Views:

  • SRSD is a 6-stage instructional framework: Develop Background Knowledge, Discuss It, Model It, Memorize It, Support It, Independent Performance

We Agree: SRSD is the missing layer in AlphaWrite. TWR gives students the vocabulary and structure of good writing (sentences, paragraphs, essay architecture). SRSD gives them the self-management to deploy those skills independently. We adopt Graham & Harris's position that the strategy itself, the named and memorizable procedure, is what transfers to new contexts.

We Diverge: SRSD was developed for teacher-led, classroom-based delivery with 6 to 8 students. AlphaWrite must deliver it fully in-app, asynchronously, to individual students. We also diverge on one self-regulation component: per Geres-Smith et al. That is a different form of self-regulation: evaluating your own work against known standards rather than narrating your own process. Our Verify It step is explicit self-monitoring instruction and is essential for transfer.

Spiky POVs (DOK 4)

Truths

SPOV Truth 1: The Strategy Must Live in the Student's Memory, Not in the App

A scaffold that exists only in the platform does not transfer to the test. AlphaWrite's current model runs like this: the SPO template is visible, the student fills it in, the student writes a paragraph (with tutorial content available), and the AI grades it and gives feedback to improve the next rep. On STAAR, no template appears, the student must self-initiate planning, and the student must write without any scaffold. The student who memorized CASE carries it into any context. The student who relied on the app's template has nothing. This is the fundamental design principle of SRSD integration, not an implementation detail. If the strategy is not memorized and automatically retrievable, it does not transfer.

SPOV Truth 2: Planning Must Be Connected to Production — They Are Not Separate Skills

In AlphaWrite G3-5, students learn to write an SPO (a planning activity) and learn to write a paragraph (a writing activity). These are independent. Students do not use their own plan to write their own paragraph, so they have never experienced their own plan improving their own draft. They don't self-initiate planning on tests because, from their experience, planning is something you do in "planning activities" rather than a tool that makes writing better. The fix: every plan-to-draft activity must make the student's plan visible during drafting and must grade the draft, not the plan. The quality of the plan then shows up in the quality of the output, and students feel the cause and effect. Planned writing is better writing.

SPOV Truth 3: First-Draft Quality Must Be Explicitly Trained — Revision Loops Alone Are Not Sufficient

AlphaWrite currently teaches writing through revision: write, AI grades, specific feedback, revise, iterate to passing. This trains students to produce quality writing with external feedback. On tests there is no multi-rep feedback. There is one shot, and students perform at their untrained first-draft level. SRSD addresses this by teaching students to self-monitor ("Did I do C? A? S? E?"), which replaces the AI's external quality check with the student's internalized one. The internalized mnemonic is the self-monitoring tool.

SPOV Truth 4: The Mnemonic Structure Is the Instruction — Not a Tutorial Students Can Skip

Struggling students skip tutorials, so teaching SRSD through a tutorial they won't read defeats the purpose. The solution is to make the mnemonic the activity structure itself. Each field in the planning step corresponds to a letter (for CASE: C = Claim field, A = Argue field, S = Support field, E = End field). Students learn the mnemonic by repeatedly doing it, filling in C, A, S, E across dozens of prompts, whether or not they read the tutorial. The structure teaches through forced engagement rather than passive consumption. Even a student who ignores the tutorial still practices the mnemonic's steps, because that is what the activity is.

SPOV Truth 5: Scaffold Fading Must Be Explicit and Gated — Not Optional

The progression runs from scaffold present, to scaffold reduced, to scaffold absent, to the student demonstrating ownership. AlphaWrite currently stops at "scaffold present"; even its most advanced activities keep tutorials available, and the only unscaffolded writing is the assessment. So the first time a student writes without support is the test itself. SRSD's Independent Performance stage removes all supports and requires strategy deployment in new contexts. The app needs hard gates where students prove they can write without the mnemonic visible. Without them, scaffold-dependent mastery keeps masquerading as true mastery.

SPOV Truth 6: Genre-Matching Is Non-Negotiable — One Size Does Not Fit All

What is non-negotiable is that the scaffold matches the genre, not the specific letters of any given acronym. A single planning strategy cannot serve all modes (informational/expository, argumentative, opinion, and so on). The research validates genre-matched mnemonics, and AlphaWrite uses real, goal-naming words: CASE for opinion (G3-5), STOP+DARE for argument (G6-8), FACT for informational (all grades). Students need the right tool for each task type, retrievable by recognizing the prompt type. The student asks "what kind of writing is this?", the matched mnemonic fires, the plan forms, and the draft follows.

The acronyms are well-chosen vehicles, not sacred objects (see Insight 9). Three things cannot be compromised: each genre gets its own matched scaffold; once a mnemonic is chosen it stays consistent, because a student cannot internalize a strategy that keeps changing shape; and it maps cleanly enough to the task to be memorable. Within those constraints, the exact letters matter far less than the method around them.

SPOV Truth 7: Self-Regulation Replaces External Regulation — The Student Becomes Their Own Grader

In AlphaWrite, the AI monitors quality and provides feedback. That is external regulation. On tests, no AI regulation exists while the student is writing. SRSD teaches self-monitoring: after writing, re-read with your mnemonic as a checklist. "Did I state my claim? (C) Did I give my reasons? (A) Did I support each reason with passage evidence and explain why it matters? (S) Did I write an ending? (E)" This self-checking loop is what keeps elaboration from vanishing under load. It works not because the student has more cognitive bandwidth, but because the mnemonic provides a retrieval cue for each element. The student checks each letter and catches what's missing before submitting.

Myths

SPOV Myth 1: We don't believe that providing a planning template is the same as teaching planning.

Providing a template teaches students the structure of a plan. It does not teach them to plan. A student handed a template fills it in. A student who has internalized planning sits down with a blank page, thinks "first I need to organize my thoughts," and generates their own structure. AlphaWrite currently provides templates. SRSD teaches the behavior of planning itself.

SPOV Myth 2: We don't believe revision-loop learning reliably transfers to first-draft contexts. It transfers slowly, unevenly, and only for students who internalize the criteria on their own.

Revision-loop learning can transfer to first-draft quality, but the mechanism is internalization, not reps, and that distinction is everything. Transfer happens only when a student stops treating each correction as a one-off patch ("the AI said add evidence, so I added evidence" is compliance) and starts anticipating the feedback before receiving it, building an internal model of the criteria. Reps neither guarantee nor reliably produce that shift.

There are three problems with betting on reps to get students there:

  1. It's a discovery-learning bet, and discovery learning fails novices. Asking a student to extract the generative rules of good writing from a series of feedback instances is discovery learning (Kirschner, Sweller & Clark, 2006). It works for already-competent students who can abstract a pattern from examples, and it fails precisely the strugglers we most need to reach, who run the loop forever treating each fix as isolated and never abstracting the rule. For them, the reps-to-mastery count isn't just variable; it's effectively infinite, because they're not on a trajectory toward internalization at all.
  1. It's a performance-versus-learning trap (Bjork). Revising to a passing grade is performance. It can improve while the underlying first-draft capacity stays flat, because the scaffold (AI feedback) is carrying the cognitive load of identifying what's wrong. Improvement in the loop can mask zero improvement in unscaffolded ability.
  1. Revision and first-draft production are different cognitive tasks. In revision the text already exists, so the student evaluates an external artifact with working memory free. In first-draft writing the student must generate and evaluate at the same time, a far higher working-memory load (McCutchen). A student who revises beautifully with the draft in front of them may still collapse at first draft, where they must hold the criteria in mind while generating. This is the elaboration-collapse mechanism of Insight 3 one level up, and reps don't automatically close that transfer distance.

The practical killer: even where reps do work, the number needed is uncontrollable, varies enormously per student, and is incompatible with a pace of two grade levels per year.

The SRSD bet is not "engaged students need fewer reps." Engagement is a real but separate variable that buys more willing reps. And the bet is not "reps don't work," since they demonstrably produce in-app mastery. The actual lever is explicit versus implicit internalization. SRSD's self-monitoring instruction front-loads, deliberately and reliably and for all students, the internalization that revision-only learning leaves to chance and delivers only to the already-able. The AI's external quality check becomes the student's internal one (see SPOV Truth 7) by design rather than by accident. That is what makes mastery survive when the scaffold is removed.

SPOV Myth 3: We don't believe that students will self-initiate planning just because they've practiced it in planning-specific activities.

If planning and production are taught as separate skills, students file them in separate mental categories: "planning is what I do in planning activities; writing is what I do in writing activities." On a test they're in a "writing situation," so they write. They don't plan because nothing in their experience says planning belongs in writing situations. The fix is plan-to-draft activities where planning and writing happen in the same workflow, every time, until planning-before-writing becomes a single fused behavior.

Knowledge Tree: SRSD Integration Layer (DOK 2)

This layer sits on top of AlphaWrite's existing Composition strand. It does not replace sentence, paragraph, or essay instruction; it adds the self-management layer that makes those skills deployable independently.

Architecture: How SRSD Maps to Existing Courses

EXISTING ALPHAWRITE (TWR) — unchanged:
  Sentences (G3-8): component skills (BBS, appositives, combining)
  Paragraphs (G3-8): paragraph structure (SPO, topic sentences, elaboration)
  Essays (G6-8): essay writing process (5-step process)

NEW ACTIVITIES WITHIN EXISTING COURSES:
  Sentences: SCAN (fix run-ons) + Sentence Mastery Gate, placed between Subordinating Conjunctions and Combine Sentences (discrimination before production)
  Paragraphs: PROVE + CASE (opinion) + FACT (informational) + COPS + Paragraph Mastery Gate, placed after Finishing Transition Words, before Write a Paragraph from Prompt

NEW MULTI-PARAGRAPH COURSE (G3-5):
  Multi-Paragraph Opinion (CASE at essay level)
  Multi-Paragraph Informational (FACT at essay level)
  ECR Mastery Gate

RESTRUCTURED ESSAYS COURSE (G6-8):
  Multi-Paragraph Argument (STOP+DARE; DARE adds the counterargument move)
  Multi-Paragraph Informational (FACT extended)
  Paired-Passage ECR
  ECR Mastery Gate

Key architectural decisions:

  1. No existing activities are removed or redesigned. SRSD activities are new additions placed within the existing course sequence. SPO skills stay as they are (they teach the concept CASE/FACT build on). Write a Paragraph from Prompt stays as the unscaffolded mastery check.
  2. SCAN is placed before Combine Sentences, so students discriminate broken joins before producing correct ones.
  3. The Sentence Mastery Gate complements existing Sentence Mastery rather than replacing it. The existing activity tests production; the gate tests discrimination and repair. Both are needed.
  4. CASE/FACT are scaffolded practice; Write from Prompt is the unscaffolded mastery check. The sequence: learn with scaffold (CASE/FACT), then prove without scaffold (Write from Prompt), then advance to multi-paragraph.
  5. Topic choice is preserved. Students choose topics and the system generates passages, matching the current model. STAAR-format assigned passages are reserved for mastery gates only.
  6. Genre discrimination is introduced with FACT, not CASE. Students need two strategies before they can learn to switch between them. CASE (opinion) is taught first; FACT (informational) is where "is this prompt asking me to argue or to explain?" is introduced.
  7. Opinion is the one differentiation point across grade bands. G3-5 opinion is CASE (no counterargument). G6-8 argument is STOP+DARE, where DARE adds the R (reject-the-other-side) move that G8 Score-3 requires. FACT, PROVE, COPS, and SCAN are constant across all grades. The CASE-to-DARE transition is taught as a named upgrade of the same strategy: End is unchanged, Claim becomes Develop-thesis (the same move), CASE's Argue and Support consolidate into DARE's single Add-support, and R is the one genuinely new move.

Prerequisites: When SRSD Activities Unlock

SRSD activities are placed at specific points within existing courses. The prerequisite chain:

Sentences skills 1-10 (Fragment through Subordinating Conjunctions)
  → SCAN (fix run-ons; discrimination of broken clause joins)
  → Combine Sentences (production of correct joins)
  → ... remaining sentence skills ...
  → Sentence Mastery Gate

Paragraphs skills 1-14 (through Finishing Transition Words)
  → PROVE (supporting detail development)
  → CASE (opinion paragraph with plan-to-draft)
  → FACT (informational paragraph with plan-to-draft + genre discrimination)
  → COPS (proofreading; taught before the mastery checks so they can include a self-check)
  → Write a Paragraph from Prompt = the unscaffolded gate (Node S8), mixed-genre: student self-selects CASE vs FACT; now with COPS self-check
  → Write a Free-Form Paragraph (existing; pure-autonomy practice, student's own topic/mode, with COPS self-check)
  → Paragraph Mastery Gate (unscaffolded mixed-genre under test conditions: one-shot, timed)
  → Multi-Paragraph Course unlocks

Students must complete elaboration and transition skills before PROVE adds value. PROVE must precede CASE/FACT, because students need the detail-level procedure before the paragraph-level structure.

Why CASE precedes FACT — a cognitive-load argument, not an arbitrary order. Two reasons:

  1. CASE (opinion) is less passage-dependent, so students learn the new mechanic under lighter content load. The hard new thing in both activities is the plan-to-draft mechanic: organize a plan, then write from it. You want students learning that mechanic while the content demand is as low as possible. In CASE (opinion), the student supplies the claim and reasons from their own head, and only the evidence comes from the passage. In FACT (informational), everything (topic, details, elaboration) must be extracted from and accurately represent the passage, which adds substantial reading-comprehension load on top of the mechanic. Teaching CASE first lets students master the plan-to-draft move before FACT stacks comprehension demand on top of it. (This is about passage-dependence, not "opinion is easier than explaining" in the abstract. Once a passage is involved, even opinion requires reading for evidence. CASE is lighter because the student still owns the claim and reasons.)
  1. Genre discrimination needs a first strategy to exist before there's anything to discriminate against. You cannot teach "is this opinion or informational?" until students know at least one mode. Whichever comes first is taught without genre discrimination; the second is where the choice is introduced. CASE-first makes CASE the "learn the mechanic" activity and FACT the "now also learn to choose the right strategy" activity, with the discrimination prompt "Make a CASE or give the FACTS?" doubling as a self-teaching genre cue.

This ordering is a defensible default, not a logical necessity. The fixed constraints are only that one mode must precede genre discrimination and that PROVE must precede both. If cohort assessment data showed informational responses to be a substantially larger failure point than argumentative, it would be worth revisiting whether the higher-stakes mode deserves the second slot (more consolidated mechanic plus genre-discrimination reinforcement). Absent that signal, CASE-first wins on cognitive load.

COPS comes after content is established (PROVE/CASE/FACT) but before the mastery checks. Conventions monitoring should not be layered on before students can produce content (the STAAR rubric auto-zeros conventions when development is absent), and the mastery checks must certify a proofread paragraph rather than an unchecked one. CASE and FACT keep their content-focused Step 4 (Verify It); COPS integrates as a self-check from Write a Paragraph from Prompt onward, so proofreading becomes a habit attached to every independent production.

Grade Band: G3-5 (Developing Writers)

Note on node numbering: The S-sequence below lists the SRSD-integration nodes in teaching order across the K-8 progression. SCAN (S1) lives in the Sentences course; PROVE through Write a Paragraph from Prompt (S2–S8) live in the Paragraphs course; the multi-paragraph nodes (S9–S12) live in the Multi-Paragraph / Essays courses. Every mnemonic in the system has its own node (SCAN, PROVE, CASE, FACT, COPS at G3-5; STOP+DARE added at G6-8).

Node S1: Learn the Mnemonic — SCAN (Sentence Repair)

SCAN means "scan your sentence for problems, like an investigator." It is a real word for the act of searching carefully. This node lives in the Sentences course, placed between Subordinating Conjunctions and Combine Sentences, so students learn to see a broken clause-join (discrimination) before they're asked to produce correct joins (production).

  • Prerequisite: Sentence skills through Subordinating Conjunctions (students must understand how clauses connect before they can spot a broken connection)
  • Type: Discrimination + Production
  • What it is: Students fix run-on sentences and comma splices using the SCAN procedure.
  S — Stop at each period or comma: is there a full thought on each side?
  C — Count the independent clauses (more than one with no proper join = a problem)
  A — Apply a fix: split with a period, OR join with a comma + conjunction
       (G6-8 adds: OR join with a semicolon)
  N — Name your fix: say in your own words why you fixed it that way
  • Why this node matters: STAAR's Writing SCR (1 point, every test) asks students to rewrite a flawed sentence, and run-ons and comma splices are the most common flaw. Placing it before Combine Sentences means students discriminate broken joins before producing correct ones (discrimination before production). The 5 testing-loop students who plateaued on sentence-combining may be producing the very errors they can't yet diagnose, and SCAN builds that diagnostic eye.
  • Scope note (avoids overlap with PROVE): SCAN targets one error type, improperly joined independent clauses. It is not a general proofreading pass (that is COPS, S7).
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably identify and fix run-ons and comma splices in connected text, without the SCAN labels visible.

Node S2: Learn the Mnemonic — PROVE (Supporting Detail)

PROVE is a real word that names the goal: a supporting detail must prove the point. This node lives in the Paragraphs course, placed after Finishing Transition Words. It teaches the single supporting-detail procedure that later nests inside CASE's S, FACT's C, and DARE's A.

  • Prerequisite: Paragraphs skills through Finishing Transition Words (students must know what elaboration is and how ideas connect before learning the cite-and-explain procedure)
  • Type: Structure + Production
  • What it is: Students develop one supporting detail fully, from a passage.
  P — Point: what are you trying to show? (the claim this detail supports)
  R — Reference: what does the passage say? (a specific detail/quote/fact)
  O — Own words: paraphrase it (don't copy)
  V — Very important because…: explain WHY it matters (the "so what")
  E — End link: connect it back to your point
  • Why this node matters: Elaboration collapse, the "so what" vanishing under load, is the most common pattern observed in coaching. PROVE isolates that exact skill and gives the "so what" its own mandatory step (V). It is the most granular composition mnemonic and the engine inside the others: each reason in CASE/DARE and each detail in FACT is developed with a PROVE.
  • Why it precedes CASE/FACT: Students need the detail-level procedure before the paragraph-level structure that contains it. A student who can't PROVE one detail will produce a CASE/FACT skeleton with hollow body content.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably produce a developed supporting detail (passage evidence cited, paraphrased, explained, and linked back to the point) across varied passages.

Node S3: Learn the Mnemonic — CASE (Opinion)

CASE means "make your case." It is a real word that names the genre: when you write an opinion, you are making a case for what you think.

  • Prerequisite: Node S2 mastered (PROVE)
  • Type: Structure + Production
  • What it is: Students fill in a CASE planning structure for an opinion prompt. Fields are grouped by letter, so students think in stages (brainstorm reasons, then develop evidence for each).
  C — Claim: What is your opinion? (one sentence)
  A — Argue: List your reasons WHY you think this (2 reasons — brainstorm stage, just the reasons)
  S — Support: For EACH reason, find passage evidence AND explain why it matters (development stage)
  E — End: How do you wrap it up? Don't just repeat — add a final thought. (one sentence)

Students brainstorm all reasons under A, then develop all evidence and explanations under S. The plan separates thinking into brainstorm, then develop. When drafting, students interleave naturally (reason 1 with its support, then reason 2 with its support).

  • How it works:
  1. Step 1, See it (two-part discrimination): Part A compares a planned versus unplanned paragraph (same length, different quality; the student identifies which is organized and why). Part B labels the CASE elements in the strong response (the student drags C/A/S/E labels onto sentences).
  2. Step 2, Plan it: Student reads a provided passage and prompt based on their topic choice, then fills in CASE fields with the plan visible.
  3. Step 3, Do it (graded): Student writes their paragraph with the CASE plan visible on screen. The AI grades against a rubric (development, organization, evidence, conventions).
  4. Step 4, Verify it: Targeted verification questions about their own draft, not checkboxes. "Copy your claim sentence." "What evidence did you use for reason 2, and why does it matter?" The AI checks the answers against the submitted text.
  5. Specific feedback and retry loop (existing mechanism)
  • Why this node matters: This is the first time in AlphaWrite where a student's own plan feeds directly into their own draft within a single workflow at the paragraph level. The mnemonic structure is inescapable; you cannot complete the activity without engaging with C, A, S, E. Students learn CASE by doing CASE in the context where they are writing, not by reading about CASE. CASE is scaffolded practice that prepares students for the existing Write a Paragraph from Prompt activity, which serves as the unscaffolded mastery check.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably produce a passing opinion paragraph using CASE across varied prompts and passages (not a one-off success on a single familiar prompt).
  • Topic choice: Students choose their topic and the system generates a passage about it (matching the existing Write a Paragraph from Prompt model). Students write about what they love while practicing the passage-referencing skill.
  • Relationship to existing SPO skills: TWR's SPO teaches the concept of paragraph structure, meaning what a plan looks like. CASE gives students a memorable deployment procedure for that concept, a named strategy they can retrieve from memory on a test. SPO says "paragraphs have topic sentences, details, and conclusions." CASE says "when I sit down to write my opinion, I make my C-A-S-E." SPO is the knowledge; CASE is the automatic procedure.
  • Scales to G6-8 as DARE: CASE is the G3-5 opinion strategy. At G6-8 it upgrades to DARE (same A and E, plus R = reject the other side); see the G6-8 band. The G3-5 version deliberately has no counterargument, which is correct for the band.

Node S4: Learn the Mnemonic — FACT (Informational)

FACT is a real word that names the genre: informational writing is about giving the facts.

  • Prerequisite: Node S3 mastered (CASE)
  • Type: Structure + Production
  • What it is: Students fill in a FACT planning structure for an informational prompt.
  F — Focus: What are you explaining? (controlling idea, one sentence)
  A — Add details: What are the key facts/points? (2-3 details from the passage)
  C — Clarify: For each detail, explain it further or give an example
  T — Tie up: Concluding statement that ties back to the focus
  • How it works: Same 4-step flow as S3 (See it, Plan it, Do it, Verify it), but with informational passages and the FACT structure. The key difference is in Step 1: this is where genre discrimination is introduced. Students now have two strategies (CASE and FACT) and must learn to identify which prompt type requires which. The discrimination prompt is self-teaching because both words name their genre: "Is this prompt asking you to make a CASE (your opinion) or give the FACTS (explain something)?" "Do you think..." points to CASE. "Explain how..." points to FACT.
  • Why this node matters: STAAR tests informational ECR at every grade. Students need a different planning structure for "explain this topic" than for "argue your opinion." FACT scaffolds the informational mode specifically: focus, add the key facts, then clarify each, which addresses the elaboration collapse seen in coaching. Introducing genre discrimination here rather than in CASE is pedagogically correct, because students need two options before they can learn to choose between them.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably produce a passing informational paragraph using FACT across varied prompts, and can correctly distinguish opinion from informational prompts (CASE vs FACT) when asked to identify the type.
  • Scales to G6-8 unchanged: FACT is the informational strategy at all grades. Informational writing acquires no new structural move across G3-8; what changes is the evidence demand (single passage, then paired passages), which lives inside the "Add details" step rather than the mnemonic. FACT does not differentiate by band.

Node S5: Scaffold Fade — CASE (Opinion)

  • Prerequisite: Node S3 mastered
  • Type: Production (scaffold-reduced)
  • What it is: Same opinion paragraph writing, but with CASE labels only and no field descriptions. The student must recall what each letter means.
  C — [blank field]
  A — [blank field]
  S — [blank field]
  E — [blank field]
  • How it works: The planning step shows only the letters C, A, S, E with blank fields. No descriptions, no prompts, no examples. The student must remember what each letter means and fill it accordingly. The draft step stays the same, with the plan visible while writing.
  • Why this node matters: It tests whether the student has internalized what each letter means rather than just filling in clearly labeled fields. Moving from "fill in the box labeled 'Argue: Why do you think that?'" to "fill in A" is the step from following instructions to owning the strategy.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably produce passing paragraphs with only the labels-only scaffold across varied prompts, which demonstrates recall of what each letter means rather than dependence on the descriptions.

Node S6: Scaffold Fade — FACT (Informational)

  • Prerequisite: Node S4 mastered
  • Type: Production (scaffold-reduced)
  • What it is: Same as S5 but for FACT. Labels only: F, A, C, T with blank fields.
  • Mastery criterion: Same as S5

Node S7: Learn the Mnemonic — COPS (Proofreading)

COPS is a real word once you frame it right. In direct instruction, COPS is taught through a detective metaphor: "You are the COPS. Investigate your own writing and catch the wrongdoers (the errors). A good investigator doesn't stop at the first clue; they search the whole scene systematically." This turns the one non-dictionary acronym in the set into a vivid role the student plays, and the "thorough investigation" frame directly teaches the behavior COPS exists to instill, since students tend to stop proofreading too early while an investigator searches everything. Keep it positive: the student is the detective, and the errors are what they're hunting (never about catching people).

  • Prerequisite: Nodes S5 and S6 mastered (CASE and FACT faded); students must be producing paragraphs before they can learn to proofread them
  • Type: Discrimination + Production
  • What it is: Students proofread a paragraph by running four systematic checks, investigating one category at a time.
  C — Capitalization: first word of each sentence? proper nouns?
  O — Organization: does every sentence belong? do they make sense in order?
  P — Punctuation: periods, commas, apostrophes correct? (use SCAN if you find a run-on)
  S — Spelling: any word that looks wrong? common mix-ups (their/there)?
  • Why this node matters: It targets STAAR ECR Trait 2 (conventions, 0-2 points). Students write and submit without re-reading, and COPS gives them a procedure for what "check your work" actually means. It also converts external regulation (the AI catches errors) into self-regulation (the student catches their own), which matters because on the test there is no AI.
  • Why here, after content and before the mastery checks: Conventions monitoring must not be layered on before students can produce content (the STAAR rubric auto-zeros Trait 2 when Trait 1/development is absent), and the mastery checks must certify a proofread paragraph. So COPS comes after CASE/FACT (content established) but before Write a Paragraph from Prompt.
  • Downstream, COPS becomes a habit rather than a one-off: From the Write a Paragraph from Prompt node (S8) onward, a COPS self-check is appended to every independent production node (S8–S12). Proofreading attaches to all writing rather than being a skill practiced once and forgotten. (CASE and FACT keep their content-focused Step 4 "Verify It"; COPS layers on from S8.)
  • Relationship to SCAN: COPS is the general proofreading pass; SCAN (S1) is the specific fix for one error type (run-ons and splices) that a student may invoke during COPS's P check. SCAN nests inside COPS.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably find and fix capitalization, organization, punctuation, and spelling errors in a paragraph, and apply the COPS check to their own writing without the labels visible.

Node S8: Write a Paragraph from Prompt — Unscaffolded (mixed-genre)

This node is the existing "Write a Paragraph from Prompt" activity, redesigned as the unscaffolded composition activity. We do not create separate opinion and informational versions; that would duplicate an activity AlphaWrite already has, and it would never test genre discrimination. Instead this single activity combines both modes: the student receives a prompt and must recognize whether it calls for opinion (CASE) or informational (FACT), then deploy the correct strategy with no scaffold.

  • Prerequisite: CASE and FACT both mastered at labels-only (S5 and S6), and COPS learned (S7)
  • Type: Production (independent performance)
  • What it is: No mnemonic visible, no labeled fields. The student is given a passage, a prompt, an optional unlabeled planning area, and a draft box. The prompt is sometimes opinion and sometimes informational, and the student is not told which; they must read it, recognize the mode, and self-select the right strategy (CASE or FACT) before writing. This is the STAAR skill, since no one tells you the mode on the test. A COPS self-check is appended before submission.
  • The planning area: The optional planning space is the digital equivalent of scratch paper. It is ungraded and carries no CASE/FACT labels, because labels would make it a scaffolded activity. The student must self-initiate the planning structure from memory. An activity with no planning space at all would be harder than the real test, where students have a scratch pad.
  • Why this node matters: This is SRSD's Independent Performance stage. It is the first time a student must both write without any scaffold and recognize the genre and choose the strategy themselves. Genre discrimination is introduced back at S4, but before this node it is only ever practiced in single-genre activities (S5, S6). This is where recognize-genre-then-deploy is rehearsed under unscaffolded conditions, the exact composite skill the test demands.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably produce passing paragraphs in both modes, correctly self-selecting the strategy, with no scaffold.

Node S9: Multi-Paragraph Composition (Opinion) — G3-5

  • Prerequisite: Node S8 mastered (proved unscaffolded paragraph ownership)
  • Type: Integrative
  • What it is: Student writes a multi-paragraph opinion response (intro, 2 body paragraphs, conclusion) using CASE at essay level. Same letters, with each part becoming a paragraph instead of a sentence.
  CASE scales from paragraph to essay:
  
  Paragraph level (what they learned):    Essay level (new scale):
  C = Claim sentence                      C = Claim/thesis = intro PARAGRAPH
  A = Argue (reasons listed)              A = Each reason = body PARAGRAPH
  S = Support (evidence in paragraph)     S = Support WITHIN each body paragraph
  E = End sentence                        E = End = conclusion PARAGRAPH
  • The CASE scaling transition must be explicitly taught: Students see paragraph-CASE and essay-CASE side by side in Step 1 (discrimination). They identify what's the same (same letters, same order, same thinking) and what's different (each letter becomes a paragraph). This is a specific instructional step, not an assumption.
  • How it works:
  1. Step 1, See it: Part A shows a paragraph-CASE response and an essay-CASE response to the same prompt side by side; the student identifies same letters, bigger scale. Part B is genre discrimination (opinion prompt to CASE).
  2. Step 2, Comprehension check (MC): 2 quick questions verifying passage understanding before writing.
  3. Step 3, Plan it: Same grouped C-A-S-E fields from paragraph CASE. The only difference is "Note to student" annotations explaining how each field becomes a paragraph.
  4. Step 4, Do it (graded): Student writes the multi-paragraph response with the plan visible. The AI grades against the ECR rubric (Trait 1 + Trait 2).
  5. Step 5, Verify it: "How many paragraphs did you write?" / "What evidence did you use in body paragraph 2 and why does it matter?" / "Does your conclusion add something new?"
  • Why this node matters: This is the first time G3-5 students write multi-paragraph compositions in AlphaWrite. The ECR rubric requires intro/body/conclusion structure even at G3. Students who have proven single-paragraph ownership (S8) now extend CASE to organize multiple paragraphs. The mnemonic doesn't change, only the scale. This addresses the most common repeated error in the testing loop (Q11 extended essay) and the elaboration collapse, since automatizing essay structure via the mnemonic frees cognitive resources for paragraph-level elaboration.
  • Multi-session design: Session 1 (about 12-15 min) covers Steps 1-3 (read and plan). Session 2 (about 15-20 min) covers Steps 4-5 (write and verify). The saved CASE plan eliminates working-memory loss between days.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably produce a passing multi-paragraph response across varied passages, reaching Trait 1 ≥ 2 (clear central idea, some organization, passage evidence present).

Node S10: Multi-Paragraph Composition (Informational) — G3-5

  • Prerequisite: Node S9 mastered
  • Type: Integrative
  • What it is: Same as S9 but informational mode using FACT at essay level:
  F — Focus/Controlling idea (intro paragraph)
  A — Add detail 1 (body paragraph 1) + detail 2 (body paragraph 2)
  C — Clarify each detail (within body paragraphs)
  T — Tie up (conclusion paragraph)
  • Mastery criterion: Same as S9

Grade Band: G6-8 (Intermediate Writers)

G6-8 students carry the SCAN/PROVE/CASE/FACT/COPS foundation forward. The single difference is that opinion writing upgrades from CASE to STOP+DARE (Node S11), because G8 argumentative Score 3 requires a counterargument, a genuinely new structural move that CASE does not contain. On top of that upgrade comes one G6-8-only addition: Paired-Passage ECR (S12). FACT (informational), PROVE, COPS, and SCAN are unchanged from G3-5.

Node S11: Upgrade Opinion → Argument — Learn STOP+DARE (G6-8)

This is the one place a mnemonic differentiates across grade bands. It is taught as a named upgrade of CASE, not a brand-new strategy.

  • Prerequisite: Node S8 mastered at G3-5 level (unscaffolded paragraph, both modes), which means the student owns CASE. A student who enters AlphaWrite at G6 without prior CASE mastery first learns the CASE backbone (Claim, Argue, Support, End), then upgrades; the upgrade framing still works because CASE is the natural floor of DARE.
  • Type: Structure + Production
  • The two mnemonics:
  STOP (the PLANNING phase — what you do before drafting an argument):
    S — Suspend judgment: brainstorm what BOTH sides would say
    T — Take a side: now choose your position
    O — Organize: pick your strongest points + note which opposing points you'll answer
    P — Plan as you write: keep developing as you draft

  DARE (the DRAFT structure — the essay itself):
    D — Develop your thesis (intro paragraph)
    A — Add support: reasons + evidence + explanation (body paragraphs; PROVE lives here)
    R — Reject the other side: state the counterargument AND refute it (counterargument paragraph)
    E — End (conclusion paragraph)
  • Why argument gets a separate planning mnemonic (STOP) but informational does not: Argument planning is a genuine two-beat generative act. You must weigh both sides before committing to a position, or the later counterargument is hollow. A single letter cannot honestly carry "brainstorm both sides" and "commit to a thesis" at once. Informational planning is one beat (decide your focus), so it lives inside FACT's F. The principle: planning earns its own mnemonic only when planning is itself a multi-beat generative act. STOP's "suspend judgment / brainstorm both sides" is also what feeds DARE's R, since the opposing arguments surfaced in S become the counterargument refuted in R.
  • The CASE-to-DARE upgrade story (taught explicitly): The backbone carries over, with one consolidation and one genuinely new move.
    • C → D: Claim becomes Develop-thesis, the same move (state your position) at bigger scale.
    • A + S → A: CASE's two middle letters (Argue, meaning list reasons; Support, meaning evidence for each) consolidate into DARE's single A (Add support = reasons plus evidence plus explanation together). This is a merge, not a new demand; the student is doing the same work (give reasons, back them with evidence), now packaged under one letter. By G6-8 this fusion is appropriate, since students no longer need reasons and evidence split into separate brainstorm and develop stages.
    • E → E: End is unchanged, the only letter that maps one-to-one.
    • R is the one genuinely new move: Reject the other side (counterargument plus refutation).
    • Student-facing framing: "DARE is the middle-school version of CASE. You still argue your reasons with evidence (now together as 'Add support') and End strong, plus one powerful new move: R, take on the people who disagree and beat them."
  • Research lineage: STOP+DARE is the validated SRSD persuasive-writing planning strategy from De La Paz & Graham (1997), "Strategy Instruction in Planning: Effects on the Writing Performance and Behavior of Students with Learning Difficulties," Exceptional Children, 63(2), 167-181 (DOI: 10.1177/001440299706300202), studied with middle-school students including struggling writers, exactly this band. The documented effect is that students taught it include counterarguments far more often than controls, which is precisely the G8 Score-3 requirement.
  • Why introduce at G6, not G8: Counterargument becomes scoring-critical at G8, but you must not introduce a brand-new strategy in the single highest-stakes year. Introducing STOP+DARE at G6 gives G6 and G7 to automate it before G8 makes it count.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably plan an argument with STOP (visibly weighing both sides) and produce a passing argument with DARE that identifies and refutes a counterargument, across varied prompts.

Node S12: Multi-Paragraph ECR with Paired Passages (G6-8)

  • Prerequisite: Node S11 mastered (STOP+DARE) and Node S9 mastered (multi-paragraph opinion)
  • Type: Integrative
  • What it is: Student writes a multi-paragraph argument ECR using evidence from two passages. STAAR's G6-8 rubric requires evidence from both passages for Score 3 on Trait 1.
  • How it works:
  1. Student reads two passages and the prompt.
  2. Student plans with STOP (the "suspend judgment / brainstorm both sides" step naturally surfaces the opposing view, which may live in either passage), then structures with DARE: D = thesis, A = support drawing on both passages, R = reject the other side (often anchored in the passage that presents it), E = end.
  3. Student writes the multi-paragraph response citing both sources.
  4. The AI grades with specific attention to dual-passage citation and to the counterargument (R).
  • Why this node matters: Without explicit paired-passage practice, students cap at Trait 1 Score 2 (evidence from one passage only). That is a 2-point difference on STAAR (Score 3 versus Score 2 = 2 additional raw points across 2 scorers). DARE's R move also directly earns the G8 Score-3 counterargument credit.
  • Mastery criterion: Student can reliably cite evidence from both passages and include a refuted counterargument in a passing response across varied paired-passage prompts.

Self-Monitoring: Targeted Verification Questions (Cross-Grade)

These are embedded in every plan-to-draft activity after the draft is written but before submission. They are not generic checkboxes (students click through those without thinking). Instead they are specific questions about the student's own draft that the student must answer, and the AI compares those answers to the submitted text.

Why not checkboxes: Students rush through checkboxes without re-reading. Targeted verification questions force re-engagement because students must produce answers (copy a sentence, name evidence, explain a connection). They can't click through; they have to re-read their own work.

Example verification questions (adapted per activity):

  • "Copy your claim sentence from your paragraph." (forces the student to identify C)
  • "What passage evidence did you use for your second reason, and why does it matter?" (forces the student to identify the S for reason 2)
  • "Does your ending just repeat your first sentence, or add something new? What's new?" (forces evaluation of E)

AI verification logic: If the student's answer reveals a gap, for example they can't copy their thesis because one doesn't exist, or they describe an explanation that isn't actually in their draft, the AI surfaces it before final grading: "You just explained why your evidence matters, but that explanation isn't in your paragraph. Add it before submitting."

Fade sequence: Full verification questions when a strategy is first learned (CASE S3, FACT S4). Reduced verification (fewer questions) at the fade activities (S5-S6). No verification at the unscaffolded activity (S8), where students must self-monitor entirely from memory.

Research Insights: SRSD for AlphaWrite (DOK 3)

Insight 1: The Transfer Mechanism — Why Memorized Strategies Transfer and Environmental Scaffolds Don't

Environmental scaffolds (templates visible in the app, AI feedback, revision loops) produce performance in the context where they exist. Remove the context and performance drops. Memorized strategies (mnemonics stored in long-term memory) produce performance in any context, because they travel with the student.

The mechanism has four components:

  1. Storage: The mnemonic is in long-term memory, so there is near-zero retrieval cost under test pressure.
  2. Self-cuing: The student recognizes "this is a writing situation" and the strategy fires automatically.
  3. Self-monitoring: The mnemonic doubles as a post-writing checklist (Did I do C? A? S? E?).
  4. Independence from environment: No template needed, no AI needed, no scaffold needed.

AlphaWrite's current scaffolds fail to transfer because they are environmental. The SPO template exists in the app. On the test, the app is gone, the SPO is gone, and the student has nothing.

In short: if a student can only perform a skill when the scaffold is present, they have learned to use the scaffold rather than the skill. True ownership shows up as performance when the scaffold is absent.

Insight 2: The Plan-to-Draft Connection Creates the Felt Experience of Planning's Value

Students who have never experienced their own plan improving their own draft have no intrinsic reason to plan. Planning looks like busywork, an extra step before the "real" task of writing. Only when students repeatedly experience "I planned this, and my writing was better, easier, or faster because of the plan" does planning become self-initiating.

That felt experience requires:

  • The plan and the draft to occur in the same workflow, not separate activities
  • The plan to be visible during drafting, so the student experiences using it
  • The draft to be graded, so the plan's quality has consequences
  • Repeated trials, so the pattern becomes undeniable

Put concretely: a student who has done 20 plan-to-draft activities will plan on a test. A student who has done 20 planning activities and 20 separate writing activities will not. The connection is the learning.

Insight 3: Elaboration Collapse Is a Working Memory Problem, Not a Knowledge Problem

When elaboration (the "so what") vanishes under multi-paragraph load, it isn't because students don't know what elaboration is. They know; they produce it reliably in single-paragraph activities. It vanishes because multi-paragraph writing adds organizational demands (which paragraph am I in? what comes next? how does this connect to my thesis?) that consume the working memory previously available for elaboration.

The fix is not "teach elaboration better." The fix is to automatize the organizational demands so they stop consuming working memory. A memorized mnemonic (CASE/FACT) that automatically organizes the essay structure drops organizational working memory close to zero, which frees bandwidth for elaboration within each paragraph.

This is Berninger and McCutchen's model applied one level up: just as automatic transcription frees capacity for sentences, automatic essay planning frees capacity for paragraphs.

In practice: if a student can elaborate in isolation but not in context, the problem is cognitive load at the higher level rather than the elaboration skill. Automatize the higher level and elaboration returns.

Insight 4: The Students Who Skip Tutorials Are the Students Who Most Need the Strategy

This creates a delivery paradox. The traditional SRSD teaching sequence (Develop Background, Discuss, Model, Memorize) relies on students engaging with instructional content. Students who skip tutorials won't engage. Yet these same students, the ones who rush through content, skip planning, and produce thin first drafts, are precisely the population SRSD was designed for.

The solution is to teach by doing rather than by reading. The mnemonic is the activity structure. Students who ignore the tutorial still fill in C, A, S, E fields on every activity. The repetition builds the mnemonic through procedural memory (the motor pattern of filling in four fields in sequence) rather than declarative memory (reading about what CASE means). This is less elegant than full SRSD fidelity, but it reaches the population that fidelity misses.

The tutorial adds value for students who read it, because they understand why. The system still works without it, because they learn what through repetition.

The design rule: build instruction so that the minimum path through the activity still teaches the strategy. The tutorial is a bonus, not a dependency.

Insight 5: Scaffold Fading Must Be Gradual and Gated — Neither Alone Works

Gradual fading without a gate lets students experience progressively less support without ever being forced to prove independence; they may coast on partial scaffolding forever. A gate without gradual fading sends students from full scaffold to zero scaffold overnight, the difficulty spike is too large, the failure rate climbs, and motivation drops.

The correct sequence: full scaffold (learn CASE/FACT, S3-S4), then labels only (fade, S5-S6), then no scaffold (Write a Paragraph from Prompt, S8, unscaffolded mixed-genre). Each reduction is a small step. The unscaffolded activity arrives only after substantial practice at the reduced-scaffold level, so it isn't a surprise and students are set up to succeed.

The minimum is three levels: full, reduced, absent. A student should be writing reliably at one level before the next removes more support, and "reliably" means consistent success across varied prompts rather than a single lucky attempt. The unscaffolded level is the real evidence of ownership.

Insight 6: Genre Recognition Is a Prerequisite for Mnemonic Deployment — But Requires Two Strategies First

On STAAR, students must first identify what type of writing is being asked (opinion/argument? informational?) before they can deploy the right mnemonic (CASE/DARE? FACT?). If they can't discriminate the prompt type, they can't select the right strategy. The real-word mnemonics help, because a word that names its genre turns the discrimination into plain language ("make a CASE or give the FACTS?").

This is the discrimination-before-production principle applied at the strategy level: students must be able to see what type of writing is being asked (discrimination) before they can deploy the correct mnemonic (production).

There is a timing constraint. Genre discrimination cannot be taught with the first mnemonic (CASE), because students need to know two strategies before they can learn to choose between them; you can't discriminate with only one option. So CASE is taught first (structure discrimination: planned versus unplanned), and FACT is where genre discrimination is introduced (opinion versus informational). This sequencing is deliberate.

The design rule: introduce genre discrimination when the second strategy is taught, not the first. The FACT activity's Step 1 includes "Is this prompt asking you to make a CASE (your opinion) or give the FACTS (explain something)?", the first time students encounter the choice. Because both words name their genre, the discrimination prompt is self-teaching.

Insight 7: Multi-Paragraph Writing in G3-5 Solves the "Paragraphs Have No Purpose" Problem

Students learn paragraph structure in isolation, but paragraphs exist to serve a larger composition: an argument, an explanation, a narrative. Without experiencing their paragraphs functioning inside a larger structure, students have no reason to internalize paragraph schema. It stays a rule to follow rather than a tool that serves a purpose they can feel.

When G3-5 students write multi-paragraph responses (S9-S10), each paragraph suddenly has a job: this one states my claim, this one gives my first reason, this one gives my second reason, this one concludes. Paragraph schema adoption improves because the schema is now meaningful; it serves the composition.

The diagnostic: if students can't adopt paragraph schema despite repeated single-paragraph practice, the issue may be motivation and purpose rather than skill. Multi-paragraph composition gives each paragraph a purpose that single-paragraph practice cannot.

Insight 8: The 20-25 Minute Constraint Is Manageable If Planning Is Fast

Once a mnemonic is automatized, planning compresses to 60-90 seconds. A student who knows CASE doesn't deliberate about what to do; they fill in C, A, S, E and start writing. This is the payoff of investing in strategy memorization: the planning that currently doesn't happen, because it feels like it takes too long, becomes fast enough to fit easily inside a 20-25 minute session.

The math for a G4 student after CASE is automatized:

  • Read passage: 3-4 minutes
  • CASE planning: 1-2 minutes
  • Write 2-3 paragraphs: 12-15 minutes
  • Self-check against the mnemonic: 1-2 minutes
  • Total: 17-23 minutes, which fits the window

Multi-paragraph writing across multiple days becomes manageable because the CASE/FACT plan is saved and visible on re-entry. The student knows exactly where they are in the plan and what paragraph comes next, with no working-memory loss between sessions.

The diagnostic: if planning takes more than 2 minutes after a mnemonic is learned, the mnemonic isn't yet automatized. Keep practicing at the labels-only stage until planning is under 2 minutes before moving to the unscaffolded gate.

Insight 9: The Transferable Asset Is the Meta-Skill, Not the Mnemonic

This is the central bet of the whole SRSD integration, and it determines where effort should go.

The bet: what ultimately transfers and compounds across a student's writing development is not any specific acronym (CASE, FACT, PROVE). It is the meta-skill of taking on a memorized strategy, deploying it independently under task constraints, and self-monitoring the output against known criteria. The mnemonics are the first vehicles for building that meta-skill, well-chosen vehicles but still vehicles. A student who has internalized one strategy through the full method (modeled, memorized, scaffold-faded, gated, self-monitored) has learned something far more durable than CASE: they have learned how to become a strategy-user.

Why this is the bet that matters developmentally: it connects directly to the TWR/TSIS progression in the main Writing Brainlift. Younger students (G3-8) need whole-task and structural scaffolds (CASE/FACT/DARE) because their binding constraint is "how do I organize a response to a prompt at all." Older students (9-12) can handle rhetorical-move scaffolds (They Say / I Say) because their binding constraint shifts to "how do I position my argument relative to others in a discourse community." The reason SRSD-built students can adopt TSIS later is not that CASE becomes TSIS; they are different kinds of scaffold at different grain sizes. It is that the student has already learned the meta-skill of taking on a strategy. You are building strategy-takers, not CASE-users. The specific scaffold changes with development; the capacity to internalize a scaffold is the asset that carries forward. (The CASE-to-DARE upgrade at G6-8 is itself a small instance of this: the student already knows how to take on a strategy, so adopting the upgraded one is fast.)

The critical implication is where to spend effort. If the meta-skill is the transferable asset, then the durable learning lives in the method (the felt plan-to-draft experience, the gradual-then-gated fade, the coping model, the retrieval checks, the unscaffolded gates), not in the wording of the acronyms. Given limited engineering and design effort, protect the method first. A perfectly worded mnemonic delivered through a broken fade produces scaffold-dependent students. A merely adequate mnemonic delivered through an airtight method produces independent writers. Don't spend disproportionate effort wordsmithing acronyms; spend it bulletproofing the fade, the gates, and the felt experience of the strategy working.

One caveat keeps this honest, because it should not overcorrect into "mnemonics are arbitrary." "The specific mnemonic matters less" is true across designs but false within one. Once you choose CASE, three things become non-negotiable: it must stay CASE, since a strategy that keeps changing shape can never be internalized (the one sanctioned change is the deliberate, taught CASE-to-DARE upgrade at G6-8); it must be genre-matched (Truth 6); and it must map cleanly enough to the task to be memorable. The claim is not "any acronym will do." It is "given a consistent, genre-matched, memorable mnemonic, the method around it is where the durable learning lives, so that is what to protect." This is exactly why the mnemonics were chosen as real, goal-naming words (CASE, FACT, DARE, PROVE) rather than arbitrary letter-strings: a word that is its own goal is more memorable and more self-teaching, which serves the method.

When forced to choose between polishing a mnemonic and strengthening the method (a better fade step, a tighter gate, a clearer coping model), choose the method every time. The mnemonic is the vehicle; the method is the engine. Measure success by independent performance on unscaffolded gates, not by whether students can recite the acronym.

Cross-Source Synthesis (DOK 3)

Synthesis A: TWR + SRSD = Complete Writing Instruction (Neither Alone Is Sufficient)

TWR provides the vocabulary and structure of good writing: what a sentence is, what a paragraph needs, how essays are organized. Component skills mastered bottom-up.

SRSD provides the self-management to deploy those components independently: when to plan, how to plan, how to self-check, how to perform without scaffolding.

Neither alone works. TWR without SRSD leaves students who know the parts but can't orchestrate them independently, which is AlphaWrite's current state. SRSD without TWR leaves students who have strategies but lack the component skills to execute them; a student who knows CASE but can't write a coherent sentence will produce a plan they can't draft from.

The integration: TWR builds the foundation (Sentences, Paragraphs, Essays courses), and SRSD adds the deployment layer once foundations are solid (plan-to-draft activities after paragraph mastery). The prerequisite chain ensures SRSD is never introduced prematurely.

What AlphaWrite Should Look Like with SRSD Integration (DOK 4)

The Activity Architecture

The dev team confirmed that multi-step activities with different question types are buildable. Every SRSD activity follows the same 4-step structure:

Step 1, See it (discrimination): Student identifies what good and bad look like. Question types: MC, tap/highlight, labeling, side-by-side comparison. Step 2, Plan it (strategy deployment): Student fills in mnemonic fields. Question types: structured multi-field input, MC for fix selection, sentence-starter completion. Step 3, Do it (production, graded): Student writes with the plan visible. Question types: text input (paragraph or essay length). Step 4, Verify it (self-monitoring): Targeted verification questions about the student's own draft. Question types: short-answer text input, MC. The AI compares answers to the submitted text.

This 4-step architecture works for all activities: SCAN, PROVE, CASE, FACT, STOP+DARE, COPS, and multi-paragraph. The mnemonic and content change; the structure stays constant.

The Full Vision

Over time, the plan-to-draft activity type expands into a complete SRSD sequence:

  1. Strategy Acquisition (S1-S4): Learn each mnemonic through repeated use: SCAN (sentences), PROVE (detail), then CASE and FACT (paragraph).
  2. Scaffold Fade (S5-S6): Prove recall of mnemonic meaning with reduced cues.
  3. Proofreading layer (S7, COPS): Learn the self-check that then rides along with every later production activity.
  4. Independence Gate (S8, Write a Paragraph from Prompt, unscaffolded mixed-genre): Prove deployment without any scaffold, including genre self-selection.
  5. Multi-Paragraph Extension (S9-S12, including the G6-8 STOP+DARE upgrade at S11): Apply the mnemonic at essay level with passage content.
  6. Transfer Validation: Performance on Noel's passage-based assessments improves.

What Success Looks Like

A G4 student who has completed the SRSD sequence sits down to STAAR:

  1. Reads the passage (3-4 minutes).
  2. Reads the ECR prompt: "Write about whether you agree or disagree with the author's opinion. Use evidence from the passage."
  3. Thinks: "This is asking for my opinion, so I'm making a CASE."
  4. On scratch paper, quickly jots: C = their claim, A = reason 1, reason 2, S = evidence from the passage for each, E = ending idea.
  5. Writes a multi-paragraph response following the plan (12-15 minutes).
  6. Re-reads using the internalized checklist: "Did I state my claim? Did I give reasons? Did I explain why they matter? Did I write an ending?"
  7. Catches that paragraph 2 is missing the "so what" and adds it.
  8. Submits.

This student scores 2-3 on Trait 1 (clear central idea, organized with evidence, some depth of thought). Without SRSD, the same student would score 1 (minimal development, limited organization, no passage evidence), because they would have started writing immediately, produced a single paragraph, and never checked for completeness.

That is a difference of 2-4 raw STAAR points. Across a cohort, it moves students across performance thresholds.
